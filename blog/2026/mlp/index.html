A two layer neural network from first principles

Detailed derivation of the forward and backward pass equations for a two layer neural network (multi-layer perceptron)

\[\newcommand{\h}[1]{\mathbf{h}_{#1}} \newcommand{\hH}[1]{\mathbf{h}^{(#1)}} \newcommand{\B}[1]{\mathbf{b}_{#1}} \newcommand{\W}[1]{\mathbf{W}_{#1}}\]

(This post is a companion to the MLP-from-scratch mini project available at https://github.com/siaahmadi/from_scratch.)

Backpropagation is the engine behind training neural networks, but it can feel abstract without seeing the math spelled out end-to-end. In this post, we’ll walk through a concrete two-layer network — forward pass, loss computation, and the full backward pass — so that each gradient has a clear derivation you can follow and implement yourself.

One habit that pays off enormously here is writing the dimensions of every matrix and intermediate value explicitly. It might seem like extra notation at first, but it earns its keep in two ways. First, it makes differentiation more mechanical: when you need to multiply two gradient matrices together, the required transposes and orderings are often forced on you by dimensional compatibility alone, leaving little room for error. Second, it turns implementation bugs into obvious shape mismatches. If a gradient comes out the wrong size, you know immediately that something is wrong. Rather than silently accumulating a numerically wrong update, you spot and correct the mistake. With that in mind, you’ll notice that every quantity in this post is annotated with its shape. Treat those annotations as part of the math.

Inputs

Suppose we have a dataset of $N$ examples, each with $D$ features, and $C$ possible output classes:

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{X} & \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times C}\\ \end{align*}\]

The Network

Our network has one hidden layer of width $H$. This means we need two sets of weight matrices and bias vectors, one for each linear transformation.

\[\begin{align*} \W{1} & \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times H}, \B{1} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times H}\\ \W{2} & \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times C}, \B{2} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times C}\\ \end{align*}\]

The forward pass computes three intermediate values. First, a linear transformation of the input. Then, a nonlinear activation. Finally, a second linear transformation that produces the raw class scores (logits).

\[\begin{align*} \h{1} = \mathbf{X} \W{1} + \B{1} & \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H}\\ \h{2} = f(\h{1}) & \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H}\\ \h{3} = \h{2} \W{2} + \B{2} & \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times C} \end{align*}\]

Note: Below, we will use the notations $\h{i}$ and $\hH{i}$ interchangeably so as to make the text more legible. In particular, when it’s necessary to index into $\h{i}$, we will use the $\hH{i}_j$ notation to refer to the $j$th element in $\h{i}$.

Nonlinearity

For our activation function $f$, we use ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit). It’s applied elementwise: negative values are clamped to zero, and positive values pass through unchanged. Its simplicity is what makes its gradient so easy to compute later.

\[\begin{align*} f(\mathbf{X}) = \mathrm{ReLU}(\mathbf{X}) & = \begin{bmatrix} \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{11}) & \cdots & \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{1j}) & \cdots & \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{1n})\\ \vdots & \ddots & & & \vdots\\ & & \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{ij}) & & \\ \vdots & & & \ddots & \vdots\\ \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{m1}) & \cdots & \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{mj}) & \cdots & \mathrm{ReLU}(x_{mn})\\ \end{bmatrix}\\[4mm] & = \begin{cases} x_{ij} & \text{, if } x_{ij} \geq 0 \\ 0 & \text{, if } x_{ij} < 0 \end{cases} \end{align*}\]

Predictions

The logits in $\h{3}$ are not probabilities as they can be any real number. To convert them into a proper probability distribution over the $C$ classes, we apply the softmax function, which exponentiates each score and normalizes by the sum. The result $\mathbf{q}_i$​ is our model’s predicted probability for class $i$.

\[\begin{align*} \mathbf{q}_i & = \mathrm{softmax}(\h{3})_i\\ \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i & = \frac{e^{\mathbf{z}_i}}{\sum_{k=1}^{C}e^{\mathbf{z}_k}} \end{align*}\]

Loss

We measure how far our predictions are from the truth using cross-entropy loss. Here, $\mathbf{p}$ is the one-hot true distribution and $\mathbf{q}$ is our predicted distribution. Since $\mathbf{p}$ is one-hot, the sum over all classes collapses to just the log-probability assigned to the correct class $i$ which gives us the closed-form expression for the loss.

Below, $i$ is the index of the correct class.

\[\begin{align*} \mathcal{L} = \mathrm{CE}(\underbrace{p}_{\text{true}}, \underbrace{q}_{\text{estimate}}) & = -\mathbb{E}_p[\log q]\\ & = -\sum_{k=1}^{C} \mathbf{p}_k \log \mathbf{q}_k\\ & = -\underbrace{\mathbf{p}_i}_1\cdot\log (\frac{e^{\hH{3}_i}}{\sum_{k=1}^{C}e^{\hH{3}_k}})\\ & = -\log e^{\hH{3}_i} + \log \left(\sum_{k=1}^{C}e^{\hH{3}_k}\right)\\ & = \boxed{\log \left(\sum_{k=1}^{C}e^{\hH{3}_k}\right) - \hH{3}_i} \end{align*}\]

Gradients (backprop)

Now for the backward pass. We’ll work our way from the loss back through each layer, applying the chain rule at each step.

Outputs

We begin by differentiating the loss with respect to the logits $\hH{3}$. The case split here is instructive: for the correct class, the gradient is the predicted probability minus one; for all other classes, it’s just the predicted probability. This unifies neatly into a single vectorized expression.

\[\begin{align*} \nabla_{\hH{3}_i}\mathcal{L}_i = \begin{cases} \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sum_ke^{\hH{3}_k}}\cdot\mathrm{e^{\hH{3}_i}}}_{\mathbf{q}_i} - 1 & \text{, $i$ is correct class}\\[12mm] \mathbf{q}_i + 0 & \text{, $i$ is incorrect class} \end{cases} \end{align*}\] \[\begin{align*} \boxed{\nabla_{\hH{3}}\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times C} \end{align*}\]

Layer 2

With the upstream gradient $\nabla_{\hH{3}}\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}$ in hand, we can now compute the gradients for $\W{2}$, $\B{2}$, and $\h{2}$, which we’ll need to continue propagating the signal backward.

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial\h{3}}{\partial\W{2}} = \h{2}^\top & \Rightarrow \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\W{2}} = \frac{\partial\h{3}}{\partial\W{2}} \cdot \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{3}}\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\W{2}} = \h{2}^\top (\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p})} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times C}\\[4mm] \frac{\partial\h{3}}{\partial\B{2}} = 1 & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\B{2}} = \mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times C}\\[4mm] \frac{\partial\h{3}}{\partial\h{2}} = \W{2}^\top & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{2}} = (\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p})\W{2}^\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H} \end{align*}\]

Layer 1 nonlinearity

The ReLU gradient is a binary mask: it’s $1$ wherever the pre-activation $\hH{1}_{ij}$​ was non-negative, and $0$ elsewhere. Backpropagating through ReLU therefore simply zeros out the gradient at positions that were inactive in the forward pass.

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial\h{2}}{\partial\h{1}} & = \nabla\mathrm{ReLU} = \begin{cases} +1 & \hH{1}_{ij} \geq 0\\[1mm] 0 & \hH{1}_{ij} < 0 \end{cases} = \mathbf{1}(\hH{1}_{ij})\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{1}} = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{2}}\frac{\partial\h{2}}{\partial\h{1}}\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{1}} = \mathbf{1}(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{2}})} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H} \end{align*}\]

Layer 1

Finally, we arrive at the gradients for the first layer’s parameters. The structure here mirrors Layer 2 exactly.

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial\h{1}}{\partial\W{1}} & = \mathbf{X}^\top\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\W{1}} = \frac{\partial\h{1}}{\partial\W{1}} \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{1}}\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\W{1}} = \mathbf{X}^\top \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{1}}}\in \mathbb{R}^{D \times H}\\[4mm] \frac{\partial\h{1}}{\partial\B{1}} & = 1\\[2mm] & \Rightarrow \boxed{\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\B{1}} = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\h{1}}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H}\\[4mm] \end{align*}\]

Note how differentiation and the chain rule become easier when the dimensions of the variables are explicit.

Wrapping up

Working through backpropagation by hand like this reveals a few recurring patterns worth internalizing. The gradient of a linear layer is always a matrix product involving the transpose of the layer’s weight or input. The gradient of an elementwise nonlinearity is always an elementwise product of the upstream gradient with the local derivative. And the gradient of the softmax-cross-entropy combination collapses to the elegantly simple $\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}$.

Once you’ve seen these patterns, extending this derivation to deeper networks and different activations and loss functions becomes largely mechanical. Just apply the chain rule!
