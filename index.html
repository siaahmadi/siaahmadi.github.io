My name is Siavash Ahmadi, and I go by Sia. Currently, I’m looking for a job in industry. I’m primiarily interested in AI Scientist, ML Engineer, or Data Scientist roles. Please visit my publications page. I’m also working on some projects to understand and improve AI systems, in particular large language models (LLMs). You can find my resume here.

I’m a computational neuroscientist broadly interested in neural computation and AI. On the research side of things, I like to make analogies between brain function and artificial neural networks and use these analogies to understand how neural networks function. In particular, I’m interested in understanding learning and planning in neural systems (artificial and biological). I also dissect systems to find inefficiencies or shortcomings and try to make solutions to address them.

My long term research goal is to define an empirical taxonomy of tasks and primitive computations necessary to solve those task, similar to how theoretical computer scientists define a taxonomy for the complexity of computational problems and the computers that can or can’t solve those problems. My long term non-research goal is to use rigorous experimental designs and large-scale data analysis to uncover hidden insights in complex systems, be they the brain, LLMs, or financial markets.

Most recently, I was a postdoctoral scholar at Stanford University where I studied how the cortex supports social decision-making in primates. There, I discovered a multi-timescale code for the computation of variables thought to be important for balancing personal goals such as reward maximization with social sensitivities such as inequity and fairness. I also dissected propagation of neural information in distributed prefrontal cortical circuits that compute agent-specific reference frames for the multi-timescale codes just mentioned.

Before that, I received my PhD at the University of California, San Diego where I worked on short- and long-timescale circuit mechanisms that support spatial learning and memory in the hippocampal formation. I described a mechanism for how phase precession is supported in the recurrent CA3 network of the hippocampus to ensure the correct storage of ordered memory sequences. I also analyzed massive (> 500 GiB) multimodal (EEG, spiking, animal tracking) data to propose an algorithmic basis for how sequence replay might support the strenghtening of long-term memories.

  1. Nature Communications
    Distinct roles of dentate gyrus and medial entorhinal cortex inputs for phase precession and temporal correlations in the hippocampal CA3 area
    Siavash Ahmadi, Takuya Sasaki, Marta Sabariego, and 3 more authors
    Nature Communications. *Open source GPU-accelerated code can be downloaded from Github , Jan 2025
    Abs DOI HTML PDF Video Code

    The hippocampal CA3 subregion is a densely connected recurrent circuit that supports memory by generating and storing sequential neuronal activity patterns that reflect recent experience. While theta phase precession is thought to be critical for generating sequential activity during memory encoding, the circuit mechanisms that support this computation across hippocampal subregions are unknown. By analyzing CA3 network activity in the absence of each of its theta-modulated external excitatory inputs, we show necessary and unique contributions of the dentate gyrus (DG) and the medial entorhinal cortex (MEC) to phase precession. DG inputs are essential for preferential spiking of CA3 cells during late theta phases and for organizing the temporal order of neuronal firing, while MEC inputs sharpen the temporal precision throughout the theta cycle. A computational model that accounts for empirical findings suggests that the unique contribution of DG inputs to theta-related spike timing is supported by targeting precisely timed inhibitory oscillations. Our results thus identify a novel and unique functional role of the DG for sequence coding in the CA3 circuit.

  2. In Revision
    Multiple long timescale computations underlie strategic turn taking in primates
    Siavash Ahmadi† and Keren Haroush
    Cell. In revision. , 2025
    Abs Video

    Successful social exchanges, from date nights to peace talks, fundamentally rely on coordination of conflicting goals or preferences. Such conflicts can be harmonized by long-time-scale strategies, such as turn-taking behaviors, however, these strategies are commonly impaired in psychiatric disorders. Despite the importance of strategic social coordination to both normal and abnormal cognition and societal integrity, its single-neuronal basis remains elusive. Here, we leverage game theory to operationalize long-term strategic coordination and dissect the computations underlying turn-taking in non-human primates. We find that monkeys can take turns and coordinate outcomes contrary to their preference. Remarkably, cingulate neurons were able to extract and encode not just instantaneous task-related information, but rather multiple, varied high-order complex features, dissociated from sensory/motor information, such as the length of the current turn and overall reward disparity over the entire session. Lastly, we show that selfish behavior is associated with poor tracking of inequity in cingulate single-neurons and populations. Our findings uncover for the first time the precision with which neurons distill dynamic, long timescale, mixed concrete-and-abstract representations of past experiences likely underlying multiple complex cognitive processes.

  3. Preprint
    Strong Scaling of Autoregressive Inference
    Siavash Ahmadi†
    . * See associated blog post here , 2026
    Abs HTML Code

    The impressive capabilities of modern large language models critically rely on generating responses autoregressively. Autoregressive token generation properly factorizes the joint probability distribution of the output which enables superior response quality. However, following the correct statistical distribution of tokens as such destines generation to be inherently sequential, bottlenecking scaling. Efforts to overcome this have largely fallen short because they fail to preserve the output’s \emphjoint distribution. Here, we factorize autoregressive generation into a latent graphical model to enable \emphstrong inference scaling. Our scheme leads to a theoretical \emphquadratic speedup. We present preliminary empirical results that this amounts to a up to over 20x faster wall clock generation time for typical-length outputs on an entry level T4 GPU at the expense of potentially more costly training. Our method’s significant gains could empower better reasoning models wherein a large number of chain-of-thought tokens must be generated fast. Furthermore, because of its strong scaling nature and that it preserves the autoregressive property, it can be combined with established methods designed to expedite AR generation to accelerate inference even more.
