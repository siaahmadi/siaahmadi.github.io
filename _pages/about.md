My name is Siavash Ahmadi, and I go by Sia. Currently, I'm looking for a job in industry. I'm primiarily interested in AI Scientist, ML Engineer, or Data Scientist roles. Please visit my publications page. I'm also working on some projects to [understand](/al-folio/publications/) and [improve](/al-folio/publications/) AI systems, in particular large language models (LLMs). You can find my [resume here](/.).

I'm a computational neuroscientist broadly interested in neural computation and AI. On the research side of things, I like to make analogies between brain function and artificial neural networks and use these analogies to understand how neural networks function. In particular, I'm interested in understanding learning and planning in neural systems (artificial and biological). I also dissect systems to find inefficiencies or shortcomings and try to make solutions to address them.

My long term research goal is to define an empirical taxonomy of tasks and primitive computations necessary to solve those task, similar to how theoretical computer scientists define a taxonomy for the complexity of computational problems and the computers that can or can't solve those problems. My long term non-research goal is to use rigorous experimental designs and large-scale data analysis to uncover hidden insights in complex systems, be they the brain, LLMs, or financial markets.

Most recently, I was a postdoctoral scholar at Stanford University where I studied how the cortex supports social decision-making in primates. There, I [discovered](/al-folio/publications/) a multi-timescale code for the computation of variables thought to be important for balancing personal goals such as reward maximization with social sensitivities such as inequity and fairness. I also [dissected](/al-folio/publications/) propagation of neural information in distributed prefrontal cortical circuits that compute *agent-specific* reference frames for the multi-timescale codes just mentioned.

Before that, I received my PhD at the University of California, San Diego where I worked on short- and long-timescale circuit mechanisms that support spatial learning and memory in the hippocampal formation. I [described](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-54943-2) a mechanism for how phase precession is supported in the recurrent CA3 network of the hippocampus to ensure the correct storage of ordered memory sequences. I also analyzed massive (> 500 GiB) multimodal (EEG, spiking, animal tracking) data to propose an algorithmic basis for how sequence replay might support the strenghtening of long-term memories.
